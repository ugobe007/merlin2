# Merlin Energy: Customer-First Product Direction

## Executive Summary

Merlin should evolve from a static quote wizard into an **AI energy intelligence and project execution platform for businesses facing rising energy cost, grid capacity, and resilience pressure**.

Important correction: Merlin is not a car wash product, a truck stop product, or a narrow vertical tool. Those can be proof wedges or campaign sites. Merlin is the broader operating layer for businesses that need to understand energy exposure, reduce cost, and turn energy uncertainty into executable projects.

The customer comes first. EPCs, integrators, and vendors matter because they help execute projects, produce quotes, and supply equipment. But the strategic pull begins with end users: businesses whose growth, margins, uptime, and real estate value are increasingly constrained by power cost and availability.

The macro pressure is becoming obvious. AI infrastructure and data center expansion are increasing demand for electricity, creating concern around utility rates, grid capacity, interconnection timelines, water-energy tradeoffs, and community energy resilience. Merlin should use this pressure as strategic context without becoming only a data center product.

The recent conversion data and GTM feedback still matter. 4,500 views and zero signups is not simply a design failure; it suggests that anonymous self-serve is not the right trust path for commercial energy decisions. But the answer is not to narrow Merlin to car washes or truck stops. The answer is to communicate a more important customer problem.

Energy is becoming a business constraint.

Merlin helps companies understand where energy is costing them money, where grid or rate pressure may limit operations, and what projects are worth pursuing next.

## Strategic Reset: Customers First

The chicken-and-egg question should be resolved in favor of customers.

End users feel the pain first:

- Rising utility rates
- Demand-charge exposure
- Grid congestion and interconnection delays
- Resilience and outage risk
- Energy availability constraints for growth
- Cooling, water, and infrastructure tradeoffs
- Incentive complexity
- Vendor quote uncertainty
- CFO pressure to reduce operating cost

EPCs, integrators, and vendors are critical second-order users. They need ProQuote-style tools to render professional budget estimates, initialize scopes, standardize assumptions, and move projects into execution. But Merlin should not lead with the supply-side workflow until the end-user energy problem is clear.

The strategic architecture should have two connected motions:

### Path 1: Energy Intelligence for End Users

For businesses, campuses, portfolios, data center operators, industrial facilities, hotels, cold storage, healthcare, municipalities, and other energy-exposed operators.

Merlin helps them answer:

> Where is energy costing us money, where is the grid creating risk, and what should we do next?

### Path 2: ProQuote for EPCs, Integrators, and Vendors

For the companies that design, quote, integrate, finance, and supply energy projects.

Merlin helps them answer:

> How do we turn an energy opportunity into a credible budget estimate, scope package, equipment assumption set, and project workflow?

The connective tissue is the Merlin Brief and Project Workspace. End users need an energy plan. EPCs and vendors need a clean path to execute that plan.

## Assumption Reset

The current strategy relied on assumptions that now need to be treated as invalid until proven otherwise.

### Assumption 1: Commercial Buyers Will Self-Serve

This appears false.

Commercial energy buyers do not casually upload utility bills, tax details, site plans, or project data into a website without trust, a relationship, or a clear business mandate. A better website can improve credibility, but it will not create a buyer that does not exist.

### Assumption 2: A Quote Wizard Creates Enough Value

This appears false.

A preliminary quote is not the customer’s real problem. The customer’s real problem is uncertainty: whether the project is worth pursuing, who can be trusted, what data is missing, what the economics are, what the risks are, and who will move the work forward.

### Assumption 3: “AI Energy Consultant” Is Specific Enough

This is unproven.

The phrase is directionally useful, but it may be too broad to sell. Merlin needs to anchor to an urgent customer pain: energy cost exposure, energy availability, utility-rate risk, grid constraints, resilience risk, incentive capture, vendor quote validation, and project diligence for capital allocation.

### Assumption 4: Product Should Lead Before Concierge Demand Is Proven

This appears backwards.

Merlin should not keep adding frontend workflow as though the website is the funnel. The buyer, pain, offer, price, and delivery motion need to be proven through founder-led outreach and manually delivered audits.

### Assumption 5: The Market Wants Another Energy Platform

This is unproven.

The market may not want another platform. It may want a trusted energy intelligence layer to answer one expensive question quickly:

> Where is energy becoming a cost, growth, or resilience constraint — and what should we do about it?

## Revised Reality

Merlin should be treated as a hypothesis, not a finished product strategy.

The near-term goal is not to build a better wizard or a larger AI workspace for anonymous web traffic. The near-term goal is to prove that energy-exposed businesses will engage with a founder-led audit offer and convert into LOIs, paid pilots, channel partnerships, or qualified pipeline.

The likely first sellable product is not software.

The likely first sellable product is a manually delivered, AI-assisted energy diligence service that produces a trusted CFO-grade Merlin Brief for an energy-exposed business.

Only after that service repeats should Merlin productize the workflow into agents, dashboards, automations, and project workspaces.

The current quote-wizard model asks users to complete a long assessment, receive a preliminary output, and then figure out what to do next. That creates confusion and drop-off. The stronger product direction is to make Merlin feel like an AI energy consultant that keeps working after the user leaves.

**New positioning:**

> Merlin helps businesses turn energy uncertainty into an executable plan — reducing cost, identifying grid and rate exposure, and moving the right projects toward engineering, financing, and execution.

Alternative shorter positioning:

> Energy is becoming a business constraint. Merlin shows where it is costing you money, where it may limit operations, and what to do next.

## Why This Direction Matters

Commercial energy projects do not fail because customers cannot fill out a form. They fail because the business problem is broader than the form. Energy touches operating cost, growth capacity, uptime, real estate value, water/cooling tradeoffs, resilience, incentives, financing, and vendor execution.

The work after initial interest is fragmented:

- Utility bills are missing.
- Tariffs and demand charges are unclear.
- Incentives are hard to verify.
- Roof, parcel, electrical, and interconnection assumptions are incomplete.
- Vendors need clean scope packages.
- CFOs need financial confidence.
- Operators do not know the next step.
- Nobody owns the project between initial energy concern and executable scope.

Merlin can own that gap.

Instead of presenting itself as another quote tool, Merlin should present itself as an energy intelligence and project system that continues working in the background.

## Core User Experience

1. User enters a site, business type, utility bill, and goal.
2. Merlin creates a live project workspace.
3. Specialist agents begin working on the project.
4. Merlin identifies missing documents, blockers, and next actions.
5. The user receives a daily project brief.
6. Humans approve engineering, pricing, tax claims, vendor outreach, and utility submissions.

The user should feel like Merlin is a persistent energy analyst and project manager, not a one-time calculator.

## Workspace Concept

After intake, the user lands in a **Merlin Project Workspace**.

The workspace should include:

- Daily Merlin Brief
- Project status
- Current recommended solar/storage sizing
- Budgetary installed range
- Savings and payback targets
- Agent activity feed
- Missing document checklist
- Open tasks and owners
- Human approval requests
- Downloadable project brief
- Guardrails showing what Merlin can and cannot do autonomously

## Daily Merlin Brief

Each project should generate a daily update such as:

- What Merlin checked today
- What changed since yesterday
- Current recommended system size
- Current budgetary range
- Open blockers
- Missing documents
- Next best action
- Decisions needed from the customer
- Confidence level
- Source trail / TrueQuote audit notes

Example:

> Merlin reviewed your project today. The current working recommendation is 240 kW solar plus 575 kWh battery storage. The project is blocked on interval data and electrical single-line documentation. Utility tariff validation is queued. Recommended next step: upload 12 months of bills and authorize Merlin to prepare interconnection questions.

## Human-in-the-Loop Guardrails

Merlin can autonomously:

- Research
- Draft
- Compare
- Track
- Summarize
- Request missing information
- Prepare recommendations
- Generate project briefs

Humans must approve:

- Final engineering assumptions
- Final pricing
- Tax and incentive claims
- Vendor commitments
- Customer-facing guarantees
- Utility interconnection submissions
- Legal or financing representations

This is important for trust. Merlin should not be positioned as an autonomous EPC. It should be positioned as an AI-backed energy consultant and project operating system.

A wizard is transactional. A project workspace is persistent.

A wizard says:

> Answer these questions, then figure out what to do next.

> Give me the basics. I will keep moving the project forward and tell you exactly what needs your attention.

That is a stronger product, a clearer value proposition, and a better fit for complex commercial energy decisions.

## Audience Architecture

Merlin should not be constrained to car washes or truck stops. Those can remain campaign sites or proof wedges. The broader Merlin audience should be organized around energy exposure and execution responsibility.

### Primary Customer: Energy-Exposed End Users

These are organizations whose operating cost, growth, uptime, or real estate value depends on energy availability and price.

Potential customer categories:

- Data centers and data-center-adjacent real estate
- Industrial and manufacturing facilities
- Cold storage and refrigerated warehousing
- Hospitals and healthcare campuses
- Hotels, resorts, and large hospitality portfolios
- Universities, campuses, airports, and municipalities
- EV charging networks and fleet depots
- Commercial real estate portfolios
- Car wash and retail service portfolios

Customer pain:

- Rising utility rates
- Demand charges
- Capacity constraints
- Interconnection delays
- Outage and resilience risk
- Cooling and water-energy tradeoffs
- Incentive and financing uncertainty
- Vendor quote complexity
- Lack of a CFO-ready energy plan

### Secondary Customer: EPCs, Integrators, Vendors, and Developers

These are the execution-side users who need to render professional quotes, initialize workflows, standardize assumptions, and turn customer demand into scoped projects.

Their pain:

- Slow budget-estimate generation
- Inconsistent proposal assumptions
- Manual spreadsheet workflows
- Difficult equipment selection and pricing updates
- Incomplete customer intake data
- Difficulty comparing options across solar, BESS, generators, EV charging, and power infrastructure
- Need for professional quote packages before detailed engineering

### Strategic Bridge

The end user creates the demand. The EPC/integrator/vendor ecosystem executes the project.

Merlin should sit between them:

> Customer energy intelligence on one side. Professional project quoting and execution workflow on the other.

The pitch is not “solar quote.” The pitch is:

> Merlin identifies energy cost, grid exposure, resilience risk, incentive opportunity, and executable project paths for energy-exposed businesses.
