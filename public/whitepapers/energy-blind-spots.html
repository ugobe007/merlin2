The Problem with "Just Pay the Bill"

Most businesses treat electricity like rent — a fixed cost you pay and forget. They optimize for kilowatt-hour usage (kWh), install LED lighting, maybe add solar. Then they look at the bill and wonder why it didn't go down. The reason: the most expensive part of your electric bill has nothing to do with how much energy you use.

The Demand Charge Problem

Your utility doesn't just charge you for energy consumed — it charges you for the rate at which you consumed it. Every month, your meter records your highest 15-minute average power draw. That number — your "peak demand" — gets multiplied by a demand charge rate (typically $10–$25 per kW) and added to your bill.

A restaurant that runs all its kitchen equipment simultaneously for 15 minutes at noon can set a demand peak that inflates its bill for the entire month — even if the rest of the month is quiet. The kitchen didn't run "more" energy. It ran it badly.

"We installed solar and cut our kWh usage by 30%. Our bill barely moved." — Common feedback from businesses without demand charge management

Five Costs Your CFO Probably Doesn't Know Exist

  • Demand Charges: Based on your peak 15-minute draw. Often $10–$25/kW/month. Easily 30–50% of your total bill.
  • Time-of-Use (TOU) Rates: Energy costs 2–4× more during afternoon peak hours (typically 4–9 PM). Businesses that run heavy loads at these times pay a massive, invisible premium.
  • Ratchet Clauses: Some utilities bill based on your single highest demand reading over the past 12 months — one bad day can haunt you for a year.
  • Power Factor Penalties: Running inductive equipment (motors, compressors, HVAC) at poor power factor draws reactive power the utility must supply but can't bill for directly — so they add penalty charges instead.
  • Interconnection Fees: Want to add solar or storage? Utilities charge variable interconnection fees to "study" the impact on their grid. In some jurisdictions these run $50K–$500K+ and can kill project economics entirely.

The Right Framework: Bill Forensics First

Before deploying any energy technology, a business must understand its cost structure, not just its consumption. That means obtaining 12 months of interval data (15-minute readings), identifying demand peaks, understanding the rate schedule, and modeling how different technologies — BESS, solar, demand response — actually interact with each charge type.

Businesses that skip this step frequently deploy the wrong solution. Solar panels reduce energy charges. BESS reduces demand charges. Generators provide resilience. They are not interchangeable — and deploying them without a bill forensics model first is like prescribing medication without a diagnosis.

The goal isn't to use less energy. The goal is to control when you use it.

Where to Start

Request 12 months of interval data from your utility (most provide this through their online portal or upon request). Identify your top 5 demand peaks — what caused them, what time of day, what day of week. Then model the cost impact of shifting or reducing each one. This analysis alone typically reveals $20,000–$200,000 in annual savings opportunity for mid-sized commercial operations.
