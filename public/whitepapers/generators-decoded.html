Three Types of Generators. Three Very Different Risk Profiles.

Backup generators are the most widely deployed energy asset in commercial real estate — and the least understood. Most businesses buy them, park them, and pray they never need them. The problems with this approach are numerous: generators sized for peak load that actually run at 25% capacity; diesel tanks that hold 48 hours of fuel in an area prone to week-long outages; natural gas "backup" systems that depend on a gas grid that may also be disrupted. Getting generators right requires understanding what each type does well — and where each one fails.

Diesel: The Default — and Its Dirty Secrets

Diesel reciprocating generators are the most common commercial backup power source for good reason: they're reliable, self-contained (no fuel grid required), and proven across decades of deployment. A well-maintained diesel generator will start and run through almost any grid outage scenario.

The problems emerge in the economics and in the risk profile. Most diesel generators are sized to carry 100% of building peak load — which typically means they run at 25–40% of rated capacity during actual use. Diesel engines run poorly at low load factors: fuel consumption rises per kWh generated, combustion temperatures are lower (leading to "wet stacking" — unburned fuel in the exhaust), and maintenance intervals shorten. The generator that "protects" you may actually be degrading every time it runs.

A diesel generator running at 25% load burns more fuel per kWh and wears faster than one running at 80% load. Most businesses have no idea they're in this situation.

Natural Gas: The "Safer" Option With a Hidden Dependency

Natural gas generators are appealing: cheaper fuel, cleaner emissions, potentially unlimited runtime (no tank to refill). For businesses in areas with reliable gas infrastructure, they're often the right choice for primary backup.

The critical failure mode is often overlooked: natural gas is delivered via a distribution network. Earthquakes, pipeline failures, and extreme weather events can interrupt gas service simultaneously with grid outages. A hospital that loses both grid power and gas service — two events that are often correlated in major disasters — has a natural gas generator that cannot start. The "backup" failed for the same reason as the primary. This is why mission-critical facilities always maintain a diesel reserve even when gas generation is primary.

Gas Turbines: Powerful but Inflexible

Combustion turbines (also called "linear generators" or gas turbines) are designed for large, steady loads — 1 MW and above. They're highly efficient at near-rated capacity, producing electricity at $0.08–0.15/kWh. They are, however, fundamentally incompatible with variable commercial loads. A turbine asked to operate at 40% load operates at terrible thermal efficiency. This is precisely why turbines and BESS are an essential pairing for any large facility considering on-site generation.

How BESS Transforms Generator Economics

  • Optimal load management: BESS absorbs load spikes and troughs. The generator sees a flat, steady load at its optimal efficiency point (75–85%). Fuel consumption per kWh drops 20–35%.
  • Cold start reduction: Every generator start is a period of high wear and low efficiency. BESS handles short outages and load transients without starting the generator. Start count drops dramatically — each avoided start extends generator life.
  • Runtime reduction: BESS can serve the building for the first 2–4 hours of an outage alone, giving the utility time to restore service. Generator runtime drops 40–60% on average.
  • Fuel security: With BESS handling most of the emergency load, a 48-hour diesel tank effectively covers a much longer outage period. The same fuel tank protects the building for 4–7 days when paired with BESS.
  • Maintenance interval extension: Less runtime + optimal load factor + fewer starts = 2–3× longer maintenance intervals. Generator capital costs amortize over a longer operational life.
A generator paired with BESS is not just more reliable. It costs less to operate, lasts longer, and protects the building for more of the outage duration your diesel tank can fuel.
