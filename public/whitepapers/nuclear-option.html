What Are Small Modular Reactors?

Traditional nuclear plants are massive: 1,000–1,600 MW, costing $10–20 billion and taking 15+ years to build. That scale makes them impractical for most buyers. Small Modular Reactors (SMRs) are a fundamentally different product: factory-manufactured, modular units producing 50–300 MW each, designed to be assembled on-site like large industrial equipment. Multiple units can be clustered to meet load requirements.

The modularity addresses nuclear's historic Achilles heel — cost overruns driven by custom, on-site construction. Factory production enables quality control, standardization, and learning-curve cost reduction. The goal of the leading SMR developers is to achieve costs competitive with combined-cycle natural gas, without fuel price volatility or emissions.

The Timeline Reality

The most important thing large energy users need to understand about SMRs: the lead time is 5–10 years from decision to power. Site permitting, NRC licensing, interconnection agreements, construction, and commissioning all take time. A hospital system or large data center campus that wants nuclear power operational by 2033 needs to begin formal planning and site analysis by 2026–2027.

The decisions being made today by Microsoft, Google, and Amazon are not because they need the power today. They are because these organizations understand 20-year energy cost structures and are positioning for a future in which grid rates are significantly higher, carbon pricing is real, and energy supply risk is a board-level concern.

  • Step 1 (Now): Qualify your load profile. Does your facility need 50+ MW of continuous, 24/7 power? If yes, begin SMR feasibility study.
  • Step 2 (2026–2027): Engage a nuclear developer for a site assessment and preliminary power purchase structure. NuScale, TerraPower, Kairos, and Oklo all have commercial engagement programs.
  • Step 3 (2027–2028): Site permitting and interconnection agreements. These are long lead-time items. Starting late means you miss the first commercial deployment wave.
  • Step 4 (2028–2030): Construction and commissioning. First power expected in the 2030–2033 window for facilities that begin engagement now.
  • While You Wait: Deploy BESS, solar, and generator optimization to reduce costs and build energy resilience in the interim. These assets will complement nuclear when it arrives.
